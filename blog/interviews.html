Interviews

Reflections on user interviews papers

Posted by Xu on Sep 21, 2019

In this blog, I am gonna talk about two papers: How Teens with Visual Impairments Take, Edit, and Share Photos on Social Media and "At times avuncular and cantankerous, with the reflexes of a mongoose": Understanding Self-Expression through Augmentative and Alternative Communication Devices . All these papers introduce various techniques for designing interview studies to capture either complex behavior (such as technology use on the go, or social preferences about technology) or presents other logistical challenges (such as interviewing people with communication challenges).

How Teens with Visual Impairments Take, Edit, and Share Photos on Social Media

After reading the paper, one of the most informative parts I found is the observation that most teens with visual impairments (VIP) tend to engage in the process visually, rahter than just rely on voiceover. This is totally different from my own understanding of VIPs, in which I believe VIPs tend to find voice user interface (VUI) is more informative. With this observation, designers who try to make efforts on accessibility deisgn has a clearer goal when it comes to VIPs -- they need to focus more on visual interface accessibility design. This is a very good example to learn from in terms of conducting user interviews.

At the same time, I also found it interesting when authors mentioned that they encouraged other researchers to expand their contributions because of the in-lab limitations. Their suggestions for further work are really informative and reasonable, which largely based on previous work and their in-lab interviews. This is very meaningful for my own paper drafting, I do learn a lot from their way of making meaningful discussions.

Interviews for ALS users in terms of AAC usage

This paper focuses on how to make improvements on existing devices via user interviews. Under this situation, how the researchers understand existing problems and design the interviews are the most valuable things I learn from this paper. The researchers first conducted very solid literature reviews to understand how AAC works and its impact on ALS users. The literature reviews help identify the problems that AAC devices face, which greatly narrow down the ranges (like the identification of TTS). Then, an interview aimed at these problems was detailedly designed and neatly conducted, which was clearly recorded in the paper in a very informative way. From literature reviews to interview design, from interview recording to results analysis, this paper demostrates the whole process neatly and that's the part I find most informative to me.
